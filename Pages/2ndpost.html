What is User Interface and User Experience?

The UI's purpose is to make the design appear user-friendly, meaningful, and attractive; it shows the visual design of a product. By interacting with the interfaces users can experience a psychological and spiritual connection with the design (Goldberg, 2022), this can be caused by its aesthetic appeal. For instance, a user can have a nostalgic feeling when they see the beauty of the design.

There are three elements to creating a good UI, the first one is a visual representation which focuses on giving the design an artistic look including aspects such as color, typography, and so on. The second one is an interactive representation that focuses on user input and allows the user to navigate the interface easily, an example is buttons that allow the user to show active participation by clicking them to move from one page to another. The third element is information planning which enables easy navigation on the structuring of the content including terminologies or search bars (Indeed Editorial Team, 2021). According to Papanek, users find symmetrical interfaces easier to understand than asymmetrical interfaces (1985: 4-5). This is because symmetry is simple and presents information legibly and spaciously. UI is the face of UX, but we need to look at UX before interfaces are designed.

User experience gives interfaces functionality and usability, it investigates the mood and experience of the user when they interact with a design determining whether they are having fun by accomplishing the goal or not and why that is so (Hassenzahl & Tractinsky, 2006: 92-93). The most important part is knowing how the user feels, what they expect, why, and if they understand the system because not understanding causes confusion which can frustrate the user. UX is an important phenomenon and the foundation of the design process as it allows the planning and sharing of ideas through prototyping, wireframing, investigating, and so on.

My suggested UI and UX

I started the design process by designing the wireframes aiming to show my initial ideas, see illustrations and explanations below.

I wanted the user to have fun, feel keen to know what information other web pages hold, and express their emotions by giving feedback; then use their feedback and ideas to design a better website. Then for the presented user interface, I will briefly explain my design choices focusing on four aspects of the visual representation: typography, colour, graphics, and layout.

• Typography: I used Times New Roman for the font because it is legible and spacious, it gives the reader a coherent text to engage with. Most people are familiar with it because it is an old font, it is neat and easy to read. I used different font sizes to suit the layout and to differentiate between texts like showing which one is the heading, this is because the reader will not be confused about where to start reading and what the content is about since headings can give the reader an insight of what they are going to read about. I also used different font weights to specify which text holds important information.

• Colour: I wanted to keep the aesthetic appeal fresh and easy to look at, I used about six colours because I wanted to be exploratory and see if the colours will be complementary. I used a colour-blind-friendly palette which includes black, white/cream-white, light-pink, light blue/aqua, grey, and dark green. I used these colours because I wanted the website to look youthful and I feel like the colours worked well together to emphasize that. For all the paragraph texts on the pages I used white because it signifies simplicity and clarity also to avoid the text blending into the background images, I used black for some texts like on the home page to signify the power that the information holds and its elegance like the captions in the slider cards. Grey was used as the background colour in the footer to show the balance between the information in the body section and the footer section. Light pink and cream white were used for some subheadings and as the hovering colour on the socials because I wanted to give the text some youthfulness and bring a smooth flow of information, and I want the website to stay neutral between formality and informality, so a little pop of colour does the trick. I used aqua/light blue to make the links on all pages stand out and show some stability and balance in the text, also a dark green background shows when the user hovers in the links to the home, data visualization, and data-art pages to show the user that they can move to the pages without hesitations and see the importance of each link. All these colours were complementary, and I feel like so far, they work well together to achieve the youth idea of the website and its informality side.

• Graphics: I used images from unsplash.com for the background images and images in the slider cards on the home page. I chose to use these images for aesthetic appeal and attraction. I still must resize and compress the images so that they do not take too much space, I used as dark images as possible on pages that have a lot of text so that the text does not blend in. Then I included graphics that I designed myself on the wireframes page.

• Layout: I was working towards readability, coherence, and order, and I feel like I accomplished this with the graphics, font, and colour palette I used to design the website. The style guide, critical reflections, and blog posts almost have the same layout because they contain a lot of text without any illustrations, so I kept the layout simple. The home page and the wireframes page have slider displays to show the content, I chose these layouts because the content includes images and carried a lot of important information for the user to pay close attention to. Also, the layout enables effortless navigation through the page and makes it more attractive.

In conclusion, my UI and UX thus far are well done, and I am working to implement them more and bring better changes to the design using the feedback I received and are yet to receive. The user interface of the website should have an intellectual, spiritual, and emotional connection with the user; the functionality and usability of the website must be investigated more through prototypes and wireframes to end up with a design with good UI and UX.
